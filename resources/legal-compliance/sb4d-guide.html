Complete Guide to Florida SB 4-D Milestone Inspections for HOAs

15 min read Updated January 2024 ME Forensics Engineering Team

In the wake of the devastating Champlain Towers South collapse in Surfside, Florida enacted Senate Bill 4-D, fundamentally changing how condominium and cooperative buildings must be inspected and maintained. This comprehensive guide explains what HOAs and building owners need to know.

What is SB 4-D?

Florida Senate Bill 4-D, signed into law in May 2022, establishes mandatory structural inspection requirements for condominium and cooperative buildings that are three stories or more in height. The law requires two key components:

  2. Structural Integrity Reserve Studies (SIRS) ‚Äì Financial reserve planning for major structural repairs

Which Buildings Are Affected?

SB 4-D applies to condominium and cooperative buildings that meet these criteria:

  • Three stories or more in height
  • Located in Florida (statewide requirement)
  • Buildings 30+ years old (or 25+ years for coastal properties within 3 miles of the coast)

What Does a Milestone Inspection Include?

A milestone inspection is a visual examination of the building's major structural components by a licensed Florida professional engineer or architect. The inspection has two phases:

Phase 1: Visual Examination

The initial phase involves a visual examination of the building's habitable and non-habitable areas to identify substantial structural deterioration. Key areas examined include:

  • Load-bearing walls and structural framework
  • Floor and roof framing systems
  • Foundation and footings
  • Balconies, walkways, and exterior corridors
  • Parking structures and garages
  • Concrete elements and rebar condition

Phase 2: Extended Inspection (If Required)

If Phase 1 reveals substantial structural deterioration, a Phase 2 inspection is required. This more detailed assessment may include:

  • Destructive testing (concrete coring, rebar exposure)
  • Non-destructive testing (ground-penetrating radar, infrared thermography)
  • Detailed measurements and structural calculations
  • Material sampling and laboratory analysis

Structural Integrity Reserve Studies (SIRS)

In addition to the milestone inspection, SB 4-D requires associations to maintain adequate reserves for major structural components. The SIRS must be completed by December 31, 2024 and must include:

  • Roof
  • Load-bearing walls and primary structural members
  • Floor structures
  • Foundation
  • Fireproofing and fire protection systems
  • Plumbing
  • Electrical systems
  • Waterproofing and exterior painting
  • Windows and exterior doors
  • Any component with deferred maintenance or replacement cost exceeding $10,000

Consequences of Non-Compliance

Failure to comply with SB 4-D requirements can result in serious consequences:

  • Local Government Action: Counties and municipalities may issue violations, impose fines, or declare buildings unsafe for occupancy
  • Insurance Issues: Many insurers are now requiring proof of milestone inspection compliance as a condition of coverage
  • Board Liability: Board members may face personal liability for failure to meet statutory requirements
  • Property Values: Non-compliant buildings may experience decreased property values and difficulty selling units

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a milestone inspection take?

Phase 1 inspections typically take 1-2 days for most buildings, depending on size and accessibility. The written report is usually delivered within 2-4 weeks.

What does a milestone inspection cost?

Costs vary based on building size, complexity, and accessibility. Contact us for a customized quote based on your specific building.

Can our regular building inspector perform the milestone inspection?

No. SB 4-D requires the inspection to be performed by a licensed Florida professional engineer or architect with structural engineering expertise.

What happens if problems are found?

If substantial structural deterioration is identified, the inspector must provide a recommended program for fully assessing and addressing the conditions. The association then has deadlines to begin and complete necessary repairs.
